My interest in Computers and Technology

Computers & Technology, Gaming, Cars, Repair, Engineering, Anime, Exploration.

My interest in technology is quite a vast one. I've used computers from decades ago and the ones we use now. It's no doubt that I've studied a good amount of these machines as far back as 2009 and 2010! The more prominent computers I used were in school, the Apple eMac and the HP Compaq dc5800MT. Teachers were using HP Compaq 2710p and tc4400 laptops, the former of which I have a model of and am typing on right now! (I have both, but the 2710p is currently active at the moment)

It wasn't long until I started taking more of an interest in computer hardware. One of the first computers I took apart was an old Toshiba Satellite laptop from the mid 1990s... I can't remember what the model was but it had a mobile Pentium MMX processor with Windows 95. While it turned on, the screen didn't light up. I didn't know why, so I went with my dad and my brother to the living room table and we took it apart. Obviously later on I'd know this was done in a sort of unconventional way, but back then those were... worthless. Now, those fetch a pretty penny on a site like eBay, and I have no fucking clue why. These are not antiques from the 1800s, they are old computers from the 90s that almost EVERYONE had.

That sole moment pretty much started my fascination with taking apart computers, old and new. Now I've factored in some other things I could learn, like how the design could have been improved and how similarities in certain computers apply to a multitude of others. For example, while the G3 iMacs were very popular and had a great look to them (for the time... and not for some), they weren't the best with internal designs. The later ones had notable issues with internal frames cracking to dust and the CRT boards completely going out. My G3 iMac in particular is a 700MHz and the CRT is about to die. If you were to design something, please look for potential faults and test EXTENSIVELY! There will be dire consequences if not, trust me.

I've worked on hundreds of computers in the past and I've owned dozens of them over the years. The things you find when working on a diverse selection of computers will be nothing short of vast. Either an overbored engineer snuck a message etched onto a motherboard (like with the Switch Pro controller), or someone was just overconfident that their "beefy" cooling system wouldn't have to spin up all the time. While the former could be a neat little wholesome message, the latter is just overconfident that something won't work. (EXAMPLE: Pretty much every Pentium 4 laptop....)

It's safe to say I have a vast amount of experience covering a wide range of computers and other technology. I might not be the best with things like, new computers and their cooling systems and designs, but that's because I don't usually have access to certain hardware. I've only had 1 or 2 things from the 1980s, and maybe something as far back as the 70s. However, I never really got to fix them or keep them for long.

My interest in games and consoles

I'll be honest, games and television/movies isn't something I take on very often. However, I do work on the electronics behind that, and I also have played a good amount of games myself. I grew up with the Nintendo 64, Wii, and PS2 up until my adolescence. By that point I just started working on computers and other hardware, and also started working on game consoles. I will say, trying to find certain parts or games for these won't be cheap! I usually softmod my consoles when possible, but I have a good set of cartridge games for some consoles like the N64 and the SNES. Disc games on the other hand? Forget about em if possible. Cartridge games are also starting to fail, but they were meant to last. Discs on the other hand, they don't hold up so well if kept in dire conditions. I'd also like to mention that your disc drives will start failing; my childhood Wii still works yet has this problem.

As for actually playing games, I've played driving sims like Gran Turismo and a good amount of Nintendo games. One of my all time favorites is Super Mario 64, as I've taken a good perspective on that game. When I was a kid, I had a lot of old stuff just... around, because my parents grew up in the 80s and 90s, and I was born 4 years after my older brother was. We had a good amount of time to get our technology needs set by then... so I'm one of those kids who just managed to cross some form of a generational gap. Super Mario 64 was very fascinating to me as a kid, the fact that you could travel through worlds in a 3-Dimensional space. Now this wouldn't be too important in a year like 2008 or 2009, but for me who's played Treasure Galaxy on a school eMac, it was quite an experience. Hell, I even grew up to like the controller. Look, I know it's not good, but it was one of the first controllers I've ever used, so cut me some slack. :)

Looking back as I'm approaching my 20s, it was fun to discover the many games on the computers and game systems I used back in the day. I played a wide variety of edutainment titles from the early 2000s and mid-late 1990s along with Nintendo 64 and Wii games, even Vizzed emulation when that was still a thing. I know a good amount of people my age who had a similar setup, and I know people my age who just fake it remembering a lot of old mid 2000s commercials while being a mere fetus.

Anyways, back to games... I honestly don't play too much of them anymore. Mainly that's because I'm at a point in my life where I need to really get my shit together, or I've just never had time for it. Basic electronics, mechanics, and engineering

This is what I plan to study in the future! Seeing as how I've identified a good amount of potential oversights in some popular computers, I feel like this could be a good way to go for a career. I've reverse engineered some very simple circuits, like the power buttons/LED board on an old Dell Dimension XPS tower from the late 90s (and I wish I kept that.) I've retraced a broken SNES controller+LED board (2 layers!!), and I have fixed NUMEROUS computers with the sheer power of a crappy soldering iron I bought off eBay.

As for mechanical things, I've fixed a fair amount of mechanical issues. Recently I fixed my grandfather's watch turner, which used a belt (like the one you'd find in an old CD-ROM drive!) and that was honestly more simple. However, I've taken this basic knowledge to a level to where I can easily fix something if I see fit. There's a long-neglected Toyota MR2 in my neighborhood... if only I could just FUCKING FIX THAT.

Engineering is something I'm still considering whether or not I have vast knowledge on. While I have managed to reverse-engineer some obviously simple circuits, something else like an old 80s computer motherboard would be a much more daunting task. I've had quite a lot of ideas, though! Recently I thought of maybe adapting the LC III internals into a Macintosh Classic, but that kinda fell under the radar now. I'm considering a career in Computer Systems Design and Engineering, though. That should be a perfect fit for me, and I can still repair stuff if I want to. I'll just be taking bigger tasks on too. ;)
